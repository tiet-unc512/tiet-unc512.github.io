    Welcome to the frontier of Augmented Reality (AR).

    Unlike Virtual Reality (VR), which creates a completely synthetic environment, AR “augments” the physical world by overlaying digital data onto it.

    To understand AR at a professional level, you have to look past the filters and games; it is essentially a high-speed optimization problem involving geometry, optics, and data processing.

    System Structure of Augmented Reality

    The structure of an AR system is defined by a continuous feedback loop between the physical environment and the digital processor.

    The Core Components

    • Sensing and Tracking: The system must “see” the world. This involves hardware (cameras, LIDAR, IMUs) capturing the 3D structure and the user's pose (position and orientation).
    • Processing Unit: The “brain” (CPU/GPU/NPU) that calculates the spatial relationship between the user and the environment.
    • Digital Content Generator: The engine (like Unity or Unreal) that renders the 3D models or data.
    • Display/Output: The medium where the real and virtual worlds merge. This can be Optical See-Through (like HoloLens), Video See-Through (like a smartphone screen), or Spatial AR (projectors).

    Key Technologies in AR

    SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)

    This is the “holy grail” of AR. It allows a device to build a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of its own location within that map.

    Computer Vision (CV)

    CV algorithms are used for object recognition, plane detection (finding a floor or table), and “occlusion”—the ability for a digital object to be hidden behind a physical one.

    The Academic Grounding

    Mathematics : The Language of Space

    AR is built on Linear Algebra and Projective Geometry. To place a digital teapot on your real desk, the system performs coordinate transformations.

    Matrix Transformations

    We use \(4 \times 4\) matrices to represent rotation, translation, and scaling in 3D space

    Pinhole Camera Model

    This relates a 3D world point \(\mathbf{m}; \mathbf{m}^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} x & y & z \end{bmatrix}\) to a 2D image point \(\overline{\mathbf{z}}; \overline{\mathbf{z}}^{\top}\equiv \begin{bmatrix} u & v \end{bmatrix}\) using a projection function \(h\):

    \[\begin{align*} \overline{\mathbf{z}} &= h(\mathbf{m}; \boldsymbol{\mu}) \\ \exists s \text{ scalar, so that, }\quad s \overline{\mathbf{z}} &= K [R | \mathbf{t}] \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{m} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}\]

    where,

    • \(K\) represents intrinsic parameters (focal length, etc.)
    • \([R | \mathbf{t}]\) represents extrinsic parameters (rotation and translation) that are computed using the camera pose in parameterised state vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\). See also: .

    Quaternions

    Used instead of Euler angles to handle 3D rotations without “gimbal lock” and with higher computational efficiency.

    Cheatsheet

    Quaternions are 4D Complex Numbers.

    \[\begin{align*} \mathbf{q} &= w + x\imath + y\jmath + zk &&\equiv \begin{bmatrix} w & x & y & z \end{bmatrix}^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} w \\ \mathbf{v} \end{bmatrix} \\ \|\mathbf{q}\|_F^2 &= w^2 + x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1 && \text{Unit Quaternion for rotation.} \\ w &= \cos\frac{\theta}{2} \quad \mathbf{v} = \sin\frac\theta2 \mathbf{u} && \text{Rotation by } \theta \text{ around unit vector } \mathbf{u}. \\ \imath\jmath k &= \imath^2 = \jmath^2 = k^2 = -1 && \text{Quaternion multiplication rules.} \\ \mathbf{q}_{\text{new}} &= \mathbf{q}_1 \otimes \mathbf{q}_2 && \text{Rotation } \mathbf{q}_1 \text{ followed by } \mathbf{q}_2 \end{align*}\]

    Quaternion to Rotation Matrix:

    \[\begin{align*} R &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 - 2(y^2 + z^2) & 2(xy - wz) & 2(xz + wy) \\ 2(xy + wz) & 1 - 2(x^2 + z^2) & 2(yz - wx) \\ 2(xz - wy) & 2(yz + wx) & 1 - 2(x^2 + y^2) \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}\]
    Advantages

    One might wonder why we don't just optimize the 9 numbers in the \(3 \times 3\) matrix directly in our Quadratic Program.

    1. Constraint Satisfaction: A rotation matrix has 9 numbers but only 3 “degrees of freedom.” Keeping a matrix orthogonal during optimization is hard. Keeping a quaternion at unit length is easy (just divide by the norm).
    2. Smoothness: If we need to estimate the camera position between two frames (interpolation), quaternions allow for SLERP (Spherical Linear Interpolation), which creates smooth, realistic motion blur and tracking.
    3. Efficiency: Multiplying two quaternions (combining two rotations) takes only 16 multiplications, while multiplying two \(3 \times 3\) matrices takes 27.

    CS : The Engine of Interaction

    From a CS perspective, AR is a challenge of latency and real-time rendering.

    Graphics Pipeline

    Understanding how vertices are processed into pixels. To maintain immersion, the “motion-to-photon” latency must be under 20ms; otherwise, the user experiences motion sickness.

    Algorithms

    Spatial indexing (like Octrees or KD-trees) is used to quickly calculate collisions between digital objects and the physical world map.

    Optimization

    Since many AR devices are mobile, code must be highly optimized for thermal and battery constraints, often moving heavy CV tasks to specialized DSPs (Digital Signal Processors).

    IT : The Infrastructure

    AR doesn't exist in a vacuum; it requires a robust IT backbone to function at scale.

    Edge Computing & 5G

    Rendering complex 3D models can be too taxing for a headset. IT infrastructure uses “Edge” servers to do the heavy lifting and beam the frames back via low-latency 5G.

    Cloud Anchors

    This allows “persistent AR.” If I leave a digital note on a wall, IT protocols ensure that when you walk by an hour later, your device retrieves that specific spatial data from the cloud and renders the note in the exact same spot.

    Data Security

    AR devices are essentially “roving surveillance” tools, capturing constant video and spatial maps. The IT challenge is securing this “Spatial Data” to protect user privacy.

    EKF SLAM

    Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping
    with Extended Kalman Filter.

    At its core, SLAM is a “chicken-and-egg” problem:

    • to map the environment, we need to know the location;
    • to know the location, we need a map.

    We resolve this using state estimation. The system maintains a Gaussian distribution over the state. That is to say that it isn’t just a single point, but a stochastic entity, with

    • mean vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) (where we think we are), and
    • a covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) (how uncertain we are).

    The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) was historically the standard for SLAM. It treats the problem as a series of predictions and corrections.

    • Prediction Step: Uses a motion model to guess the new state based on sensors like accelerometers.
    • Correction Step: Uses a measurement model to compare what the camera sees vs. what it expected to see based on the map.

    Because the real world is non-linear (rotations involve sines and cosines), we use the Jacobian matrix \(H\) to linearize these functions at the current estimate. The Jacobian represents the partial derivatives of the frame with respect to the state:

    \[\begin{align*} H &= \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}} {\partial \boldsymbol{\mu}} \end{align*}\]

    The State Vector

    In SLAM, we define a state vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_t\) that contains both the robot’s pose, i.e. position \(\mathbf{p}; \mathbf{p}^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} x & y & z \end{bmatrix}\) plus orientation quaternion \(\mathbf{q}; \mathbf{q}^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} q_w&q_x&q_y&q_z \end{bmatrix}\); and the coordinates of all landmarks \(\{\mathbf{m}_1, \mathbf{m}_2, \dots, \mathbf{m}_n\}\) discovered so far:

    \[\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{\mu}_t &= \left[ \underbrace{x, y, z, q_w, q_x, q_y, q_z}_{\text{Camera Pose}}, \underbrace{m_{1,x}, m_{1,y}, m_{1,z}, \dots, m_{n,z}}_{\text{Map Landmarks}} \right]^{\top} \end{align*}\]

    A 3D AR camera gives us a 2D projection \(\mathbf{z}; \mathbf{z}^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} u & v \end{bmatrix}\) of a 3D landmark \(\mathbf{m}; \mathbf{m}^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} m_x&m_y&m_z \end{bmatrix}\). Also, typically, the device has an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU). We use the integrated accelerations and angular velocities as the control input \(\mathbf{u}\).

    The goal is to find the posterior probability of the state given all observations \(\mathbf{z}\) and control inputs \(\mathbf{u}\):

    \[\begin{align*} P \left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_t | \mathbf{z}_{1:t}, \mathbf{u}_{1:t} \right) \end{align*}\]

    The Motion Model

    In this section, we shall discuss the prediction of camera pose using newtonian physics.

    In a 3D AR system, the Motion Model is the mathematical “prophet.” It predicts where the device will be in the next millisecond before the sensors even have a chance to confirm it.

    When we move a smartphone or headset, the prediction model handles two distinct types of data: Deterministic change (the physics of movement) and Stochastic noise (the “jitter” or uncertainty of that movement).

    Defining the 3D State

    For the sake of clarity, we augment the camera pose \(\mathbf{x}\) with the velocity \(\mathbf{v}\) and angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\) terms, so that \(\mathbf{x}_t^{\top} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{p}_t & \mathbf{q}_t & \mathbf{v}_t & \boldsymbol{\omega}_t \end{bmatrix}\).

    State Transition Function \(f\)

    Constant Velocity (or Acceleration) Model: Most AR systems use a Constant Velocity or Constant Acceleration model, i.e. between time \(t-1\) and time \(t\), the device continues its current trajectory unless a force (user) changes it.

    The Linear Translation

    follows Newtonian physics (τ is clock time):

    \[\begin{align*} \overline{\mathbf{p}}_t &= \mathbf{p}_{t-1} + \mathbf{v}_{t-1} \Delta \tau + \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{a}_{t-1} (\Delta \tau)^2 \end{align*}\]
    The Rotational Update

    Recall quaternion rotations aren’t additive; they are multiplicative. To predict the new orientation \(\mathbf{q}_t\), we multiply the previous orientation by a “rotation delta” derived from the angular velocity \(\boldsymbol{\omega}\):

    \[\begin{align*} \overline{\mathbf{q}}_t &= \mathbf{q}_{t-1} \otimes \boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1} \Delta \tau \end{align*}\]

    \(\otimes\) denotes quaternion multiplication.

    The function \(f\):

    The notation \(f\) has not been explicitly defined here. However, it is implied that the transition of camera pose, as defined using the estimates of position and rotation, put together constitute the state transition function \(f\).

    E.g. To compute the Jacobian, \(J_t = \partial f/\partial \boldsymbol{\mu}_{t-1}\), we fill the matrix per element, i.e. \(\partial \overline{x}_t/\partial x_{t-1} = 1\) and so forth.

    The Role of the IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)

    In Information Technology and CS implementation, we don’t just “guess” the velocity. We use High-Frequency Ded-Reckoning (Deduced Reckoning).

    • Cameras usually run at 30–60 Hz (slow).
    • IMUs (Accelerometers/Gyroscopes) run at 1000 Hz+ (fast).

    The motion model uses the IMU to “fill in the gaps.” If the camera hasn’t processed a new frame yet, the EKF uses the IMU’s high-speed data to integrate the motion and update the predicted state. This is an attempt to ensure that when a user turns his/her head, the digital AR object stays “pinned” to the real world without lagging.

    Predicting Uncertainty (The Covariance Growth)

    This is the “Math” that makes EKF work. Every time we predict a new position, we admit we might be wrong. We propagate the uncertainty matrix \(\Sigma\) using the Process Noise matrix \(Q\).

    \[\begin{align*} \overline{\Sigma}_t &= F_t \Sigma_{t-1} F_t^{\top} + Q_t \end{align*}\]
    • \(F_t\), The State Transition Jacobian: Computed as \(F_{t} = (\partial f/\partial \boldsymbol{\mu}_{t-1})\). This matrix describes how a small error in the previous velocity leads to a large error in the current position over time.
    • \(Q_t\), Process Noise: This represents “unmodeled” events, like a sudden twitch of the user's hand or vibrations.

    Why this matters for AR?

    If you walk 10 metres without the camera seeing any recognizable 3D landmarks, the “prediction” continues, but the uncertainty ellipsoids (the “bubbles” of where you might be) grow larger and larger. This is known as Drift.

    Mathematically, the system is waiting for a Measurement Update (seeing a landmark; ) to “collapse” those bubbles back down to a precise point.

    A Note on the Notation

    There’s a bar over predicted values uncertainty \(\overline{\Sigma}_{t}\), position \(\overline{\mathbf{p}}_t\), and rotation \(\overline{\mathbf{q}}_t\). The bar signifies the “a priori” (before the fact) estimate. It represents the system's state of knowledge after the motion model has been applied but before the sensor (camera) has looked at the world. This is a standard mathematical notation used to distinguish between a prediction and a correction, in the context of the EKF SLAM.

    Once the camera captures a landmark and performs the Correction Step, the bar is dropped. And, \(\Sigma_t\) (without the bar) represents the “a posteriori” (after the fact) estimate.

    The lifecycle of the covariance matrix in one AR frame looks like this:

    1. \(\Sigma_{t-1}\): Where we were at the end of the last frame (confident)
    2. \(\overline{\Sigma}_{t}\): Where we think we are after moving (less confident/higher uncertainty).
    3. \(\Sigma_t\): Where we actually are after seeing a landmark (confident again).

    And so forth for position \(\mathbf{p}\), and rotation \(\mathbf{q}\).

    3D Measurement (Projection Matrix)

    Recall the Pinhole Camera Model . We had defined a projection function \(h(\,\cdot\,;\boldsymbol{\mu})\) parameterised by the state vector, to compute the projection of a 3D point in our geometric model onto the camera image space.

    Measurement Residual (Innovation)

    The core of the 3D measurement step isn’t just knowing where a point projects, but calculating the Innovation \(\mathbf{y}_t\). This is the “error signal” that drives the filter:

    \[\begin{align*} \mathbf{y}_{i,t} &= \mathbf{z}_{i,t} - h(\overline{\mathbf{m}}_{i,t}; \overline{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_t) && \text{\ldots individual points.} \\ Y_t &= Z_t - h(\overline{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_t) && \text{\ldots all points stacked.} \end{align*}\]

    In AR, if \(\mathbf{z}_{i,t}\) is the actual location of a corner on the screen, and \(h(\overline{\mathbf{m}}_{i,t}; \bar{\mu}_t)\) is where the model thought that corner should be, \(\mathbf{y}_{i,t}\) is a 2D vector in pixel space.

    Measurement Jacobian

    \[\begin{align*} H &= \frac {\partial \text{(2D pixels on screen)}} {\partial \text{(3D landmarks in state)}} \\ H_t &= \left. \frac {\partial h} {\partial \boldsymbol{\mu}} \right\vert_{\boldsymbol{\mu} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_t} \end{align*}\]

    Measurement Covariance (Innovation Covariance)

    Similar to covariance growth, we apply the measurement jacobian \(H_t = \partial h/\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_{t}\) and add sensor noise \(R_t\) to compute the innovation covariance.

    \[\begin{align*} S_{t} &= H_t\overline{\Sigma}_tH_t^{\top} + R_t \end{align*}\]

    where,

    • \(H_t\) (The Jacobian) : This maps our 3D uncertainty into 2D pixel uncertainty;
    • \(R_t\) (Sensor Noise) : This is the inherent “jitter” in the camera’s CMOS sensor, (or the sub pixel error of the feature detector).

    Mathematically, \(S_t\) represents the total uncertainty of the measurement. If \(S_t\) is large, the system knows the measurement is unreliable.

    Measurement Update

    \[\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{\mu}_t &= \overline{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_t + K_t Y_t && \text{\ldots Camera Pose and Map Update.} \\ \Sigma_t &= (I-K_tH_t)\overline{\Sigma}_t && \text{\ldots Covariance Collapse.}\\ K_t &= \overline{\Sigma}_t H_t^{\top} S_t^{-1} && \text{\ldots Kalman Gain.} \end{align*}\]

    Let’s recap here, we have computed,

    • \(\overline{\boldsymbol{\mu}}\), the predicted pose,
    • \(\overline{\Sigma}\), the uncertainty in pose,
    • \(Y\) the residual, which may also be interpreted as correction vector,
    • \(H\), the sensitivity: Defines how much a change in your 3D position actually shows up on the sensor,

    So we define,
    \(K\), a trust factor: i.e. how much of that visual change we actually believe; and this is known as “Kalman Gain.” In some sense, this is analogous to information gain (as in information theory.)

    The update in state vector is straight forward,

    \[\begin{align*} \underbrace{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_{\substack{\text{Corrected} \\ \text{State}}} &= \underbrace{\overline{\boldsymbol{\mu}}}_{\substack{ \text{Predicted} \\ \text{State}}} + \underbrace{K}_{\text{Trust}} \; \underbrace{Y}_{\substack{\text{Correction} \\ \text{Vector}}} \end{align*}\]

    In case of covariance collapse we’ll look up to information theory for our intuition. In Information Theory, Entropy (uncertainty) is reduced by Information. The “1” represents the total uncertainty, a moment ago \((100\%)\). The \(KH\) represents the slice of that uncertainty that has been “explained away” or “resolved” by the camera looking at the landmark.

    Effectively, \((I-KH)\) acts as a “Certainty Multiplier” that’s always between \(0\) and \(1\).

    Outlier Rejection

    In a real-world IT implementation, we add a “gate” before the correction happens. If the innovation \(\mathbf{y}_t\) is too large, it might be a false match (e.g, the camera mistook a speck of dust for a known landmark). The system uses the Mahalanobis Distance:

    \[\begin{align*} d^2 &= \mathbf{y}_t^{\top} S_t^{-1} \mathbf{y}_t \end{align*}\]

    If \(d^2\) exceeds a certain threshold (usually based on a \(\chi^2\) distribution), the IT layer discards the measurement entirely to prevent the 3D map from being corrupted by “garbage data.”

    Why is 3D EKF “brittle?”

    In a 3D environment, the number of landmarks grows exponentially. A single room might have 500 “feature points” (corners of tables, patterns on rugs).

    • The Matrix Problem: Since the Covariance Matrix \(\Sigma\) is \((7+3n)\times(7+3n)\), having 500 landmarks results in a matrix with over 2.25 million elements.
    • The Inversion: The EKF requires inverting a matrix during the Kalman Gain calculation. Doing this at 60 frames per second on a mobile processor is mathematically exhausting.

    The IT/CS Solution: To maintain 3D consistency without crashing the processor, modern AR uses “Local Bundle Adjustment.” Instead of a global EKF for the whole building, it only runs the intense optimization on a “sliding window” of the most recent 10–20 camera frames and the landmarks visible within them.

    SLAM with Factor Graphs and Optimisation

    Modern AR (like ARKit or ARCore) usually moves away from EKF in favor of Graph-Based SLAM or Bundle Adjustment.

    Factor Graphs represent a shift from the “moment-to-moment” filter (like the EKF) to a more “global” optimization approach. While an EKF only remembers the “now” (\(\boldsymbol{\mu}_t\)), a Factor Graph remembers the entire history of the trajectory and all landmark observations.

    Introduction

    Factor Graphs

    A Factor Graph is a Bipartite Graph used to represent the mathematical structure of a SLAM problem. It consists of two types of nodes:

    • Variable Nodes (Circles): These represent the things we want to estimate, such as the camera poses \(\{\mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_n\}\) and the landmark positions \(\{\mathbf{m}_1, \mathbf{m}_2, \dots, \mathbf{m}_m\}\).
    • Factor Nodes (Squares): These represent constraints or measurements. They connect the variables and define the mathematical relationship (and uncertainty) between them. E.g. the factor node connected to \(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{m}_j\) would store the detail of the image projection(s) \(\mathbf{z}_{ij}\) and measurement uncertainty \(\Sigma_{ij}\).

    Physical Intuition: A system of springs

    Imagine every camera pose and landmark is a physical ball. A Factor is like a spring connecting them:

    • Odometry Factor: A spring between two consecutive poses (\(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\)). It says, “The IMU thinks these two balls should be 1 metre apart.”
    • Landmark Factor: A spring between a pose (\(\mathbf{x}_i\)) and a landmark (\(\mathbf{m}_j\)). It says, “The camera saw this landmark at this specific angle.”
    • Prior Factor: An anchor holding the very first pose (\(\mathbf{x}_0\)) to the origin.

    The “goal” of the Factor Graph is to find the configuration of balls that puts the least amount of tension on all the springs combined.

    Nonlinear Least Squares

    We try to minimize the “re-projection error”—the difference between where a 3D point is projected on the 2D screen and where the camera actually detected it. This is solved using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm, which minimizes a cost function \(\mathcal{L}\):

    \[\begin{align*} \mathcal{L} &= \sum_{i,j} \lVert \mathbf{z}_i - h(\mathbf{m}_j; \mathbf{x}_i) \rVert_{\Sigma_{ij}}^2 && \text{\ldots individual points} \\ &= \lVert Z - h(\boldsymbol{\mu}) \rVert_{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^2 && \text{\ldots all points stacked} \end{align*}\]

    where, \(h(\mathbf{m}_j; \mathbf{x}_i)\) projects a map point \(\mathbf{m}_j\) into a camera pose \(\mathbf{x}_i\). And \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) is the factor graph (a bipartite graph) containing camera poses and landmarks.

    What does the subscript \(\Sigma_{ij}\) mean?

    Here, we use what is known as the Mahalonobis Norm: \(\|\mathbf{e}\| _{\Sigma}^2 = \mathbf{e}^{\top} \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{e}\). With the standard Frobenius Norm, (aka \(L_2\) Norm), \(\|\mathbf{e}\|_F^2 = \mathbf{e}^{\top}\mathbf{e}\), but the \(\Sigma\) in Mahalonobis Norm get sandwiched between the vectors as covariance matrix and allows for uncertainty factor.

    Physical Intuition: “Stiffness of the Spring”

    Physically speaking, \(\Sigma_{ij}\) represents how much a specific spring is allowed to stretch.

    • Small \(\Sigma_{ij}\) (High Certainty): This is a stiff steel cable. If the sensor is very accurate (like a high-end LiDAR), the optimizer is forbidden from “stretching” this connection. The error \(e\) is multiplied by a massive \(\Sigma_{ij}^{-1}\), making even tiny errors “cost” a lot.
    • Large \(\Sigma_{ij}\) (Low Certainty): This is a loose rubber band. If the sensor is noisy (like a camera in the dark), the optimizer is allowed to stretch or ignore this measurement because the “cost” of the error is minimized by the small \(\Sigma_{ij}^{-1}\).
    The CS/IT angle

    • CS:

      We solve this by finding the point where the gradient of the total cost is zero. We use iterative methods because the “springs” (the \(h(x)\) functions) are non-linear.

    • IT:

      This optimization is the “Heavy Lifting” of AR. While Ded-Reckoning happens at 1000Hz, this optimisation might only happen at 10-30Hz because it has to solve for the entire map at once.

    The Isotropic Assumption

    In many “standard” AR implementations, we assume the noise is Isotropic (the same in \(u\) and \(v\) directions). In this case, \(\Sigma\) for a single observation is a simple \(2 \times 2\) diagonal matrix:

    \[\begin{align*} \Sigma_{ij} &= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^2 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma^2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}\]

    Here, \(\sigma^2\) is usually the Variance of the Feature Detector (often around \(\sfrac14\) to \(1\) sq.pixels.)

    Its effect on computation: The “Whitened” Residual

    When we compute the cost for a single factor, we “whiten” the error vector before squaring it. If \(\mathbf{e}_{ij} = \mathbf{z}_{ij} - h(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{m}_j)\) , the weighted error calculation looks like this:

    • Calculate the raw error: \(\mathbf{e}_{ij} = \begin{bmatrix} \Delta u \\ \Delta v \end{bmatrix}\)
    • Apply the stiffness: We don't just multiply; we use the inverse \(\Sigma_{ij}^{-1}\).
    • Compute the Cost: \(\text{Cost}_{ij} = \mathbf{e}_{ij}^{\top} \Sigma_{ij}^{-1} \mathbf{e}_{ij}\)

    If \(\Sigma\) is just a scalar variance \(\sigma^2\), then informally speaking, this simplifies to:

    \[\begin{align*} \text{cost}_{ij} &= \frac{(\Delta u)^2 + (\Delta v)^2}{\sigma^2} \end{align*}\]
    Effectively speaking,

    Under the isotropic assumption, the complex looking Mahalonobis norm in the cost function (in ) boils down to a weight-adjusted Frobenius norm, as follows:

    \[\begin{align*} \mathcal{L} &= \sum_{i,j} \|\mathbf{e}_{ij}\|_{\Sigma_{ij}}^2 = \sum_{i,j} \mathbf{e}_{ij}^{\top} {\Sigma_{ij}}^{-1} \mathbf{e}_{ij} \\ &= \sum_{i,j} \frac{1}{\sigma_{ij}^2} \|\mathbf{e}_{ij}\|_{F}^2 = \sum_{i,j} \frac{1}{\sigma_{ij}^2} \mathbf{e}_{ij}^{\top} \mathbf{e}_{ij} && \text{\ldots under isotropic assumption.} \end{align*}\]

    Background in Graph Theory

    What is a graph?

    A graph is a mathematical structure used to model pairwise relations between objects, consisting of a set of vertices (or nodes/dots) and a set of edges (lines) connecting those vertices. Formally denoted as \(G = (V,E)\), it maps how entities (vertices) are connected (edges).

    Key Concepts

    • Vertices \(V\): The nodes or dots representing objects.
    • Edges \(E\): The lines connecting two vertices, representing relationships.
    • Simple Graph: A graph with no loops (edges connecting a vertex to itself) and no multiple edges between the same two vertices.
    • Undirected Graph: A graph where edges have no direction, meaning the relation is two-way (e.g., \(A\) connects to \(B\), \(B\) connects to \(A\)).
    • Directed Graph (Digraph): A graph where edges have directions (arrows), indicating a one-way relationship.
    • Adjacent Vertices: Two vertices that are joined by an edge.

    Common Examples

    • Social Network: People (vertices) and friendships (edges).
    • GPS/ Map: Cities (vertices) and roads (edges).
    • Internet: Webpages (vertices) and hyperlinks (directed edges).

    Graphs are powerful for modeling non-linear, complex networks, unlike linear data structures.

    What’s mathematical about this?

    1. Formality: \(V\equiv \{ v_{i} : 0 < i \leqslant N \}\) is a set of vertices \(v_i\) — in its strictest mathematical sense. And each edge, \(e_{j}\) is a subset of vertices, so that the set of edges \(E\equiv \{e_j : 0 < j \leqslant M \}\) is a also a set, albeit that of subsets of verts.
    2. Tangibility:
      • Degree of a node: The number of edges connected to a vertex.
      • Adjacency Matrix: Graphs can be represented mathematically as a matrix (a grid of numbers), allowing for linear algebra to analyze their properties.
      • Path and Cycle detection: Mathematical formulas exist to determine if a path exists between nodes (Euler paths, Hamilton circuits)
    3. Invariants and Combinatorics: Graph theory focuses on invariants—properties that stay the same regardless of how a graph is drawn.
      • Handshaking Lemma: A fundamental theorem stating that the sum of all vertex degrees is always twice the number of edges, which means the number of odd-degree vertices must be even.
      • Planarity: A graph is planar if it can be drawn on a plane without edges crossing, a concept deeply rooted in topology.
    4. Mathematical Origin: Graph theory was invented by Leonhard Euler to solve the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem. Euler recognized that the physical layout was irrelevant, and only the topological structure of nodes and connections mattered.
    5. Relationship to Reality: While it is used to model social networks or transport systems, the rules governing connectivity are derived from combinatorics, not intuition.

    In summary, graph structures are mathematical because they are discrete systems that follow strict algebraic and topological laws. They are used to model the real world, but the analysis is based on mathematical principles rather than human subjective interpretation.

    What’s a bipartite graph?

    A bipartite graph is a set of graph vertices partitioned into two disjoint sets (\(V_1\) and \(V_2\)) such that every edge connects a vertex in \(V_1\) to one in \(V_2\). No edges exist between vertices within the same set. They are 2-colorable and contain no odd-length cycles.

    Key Characteristics and Properties

    • Vertex Partitioning: The graph's vertices can be divided into two independent, disjoint sets, say \(V_{1}\) and \(V_{2}\).
    • Edge Restriction: All edges must connect a vertex from set \(V_1\) to a vertex in set \(V_2\). No edges are allowed between vertices within the same set.
    • 2-Colorability: A graph is bipartite if and only if it can be colored using only two colors, such that no two adjacent vertices share the same color.
    • Odd Cycles: A graph is bipartite if and only if it contains no odd-length cycles.
    • Complete Bipartite Graph (\(K_{m,n}\)): A specific type where every vertex in set \(V_1\) (size \(m\)) is connected to every vertex in set \(V_2\) (size \(n\)).

    Examples in Daily Life

    • Job Matching: One set is employers, the other is job openings. Edges connect employers to jobs they offer.
    • Recommendation Systems: One set of nodes is users, the other is products. Edges represent a user purchasing or rating a product.

    How to Identify a Bipartite Graph?

    • Breadth-First Search (BFS) or Depth-First Search (DFS): Assign a colour to a vertex, then its neighbours to a different colour, alternating colours. If you find two adjacent vertices with the same colour, it is not bipartite.
    • Odd Cycle Test: If the graph has an odd-length cycle, it is not bipartite.

    Background in Optimisation Theory

    Quadratic Programming

    \[\begin{align*} f(\mathbf{x}) &= \frac12 \mathbf{x}^{\top}Q\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{c}^{\top}\mathbf{x} && \text{\ldots Standard Quadratic Cost Function.} \\ \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}f(\mathbf{x}_{*}) &= Q\mathbf{x}_* + c = 0 \\ \implies \mathbf{x}_* &= -Q^{-1}\mathbf{c} \end{align*}\]
    The Concept

    QP is the study of finding the minimum of a “perfect” quadratic bowl. In optimization, we love quadratics because they have a single global minimum that can be found in exactly one step.

    The Mathematical Form

    A standard quadratic cost function looks like: \(f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^{\top} Q \mathbf{x} + c^{\top} \mathbf{x}\) , where \(Q\) (The Hessian): Defines the “shape” and “stiffness” of the bowl; and \(\mathbf{c}\) (The Linear Trend): Defines where the bowl is centred.

    The Solution

    To find the bottom, we take the gradient and set it to zero: \(\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_*) = Q\mathbf{x}_* + c = 0 \implies \mathbf{x}_* = -Q^{-1}c\).

    Physical Intuition

    In a Factor Graph, if all the “springs” were linear (like literal metal springs following \(F=kx\)), the entire SLAM problem would be a single QP. We would solve it once and be done.

    Gauss-Newton approximation

    Let’s take the context of camera re-projection error. And the cost function be,

    \[\begin{align*} f(\mathbf{x}) &= \frac12 \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) && \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{z} - h(\mathbf{x}) \\ \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) &= \left( \nabla \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \right)^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) = J^{\top}\mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) && J = \nabla \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \\ \nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x}) &= \underbrace{\left( \nabla \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \right)^{\top} \nabla \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})} _{J^{\top}J} + \underbrace{ \left(\sum_i e_i(\mathbf{x}) \nabla^2e_i(\mathbf{x}) \right) } _{\text{ignored assuming } \forall i,\, e_i \to 0} \\ f(\mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x}) &\approx f(\mathbf{x}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{x})^{\top} \Delta \mathbf{x} + \frac12 \Delta \mathbf{x}^{\top} \nabla^2f(\mathbf{x}) \Delta \mathbf{x} && \text{(Taylor Expansion)} \end{align*}\]
    The Concept

    The real world isn't a perfect bowl. The camera projection \(h(x)\) is full of sines, cosines, and divisions. Gauss-Newton is the bridge that approximates a non-linear problem as a sequence of QPs.

    The Logic

    Instead of trying to solve the complex landscape all at once, we look at our current position and ask: “If this landscape were a quadratic bowl right here, where would the bottom be?”

    1. Linearise: We take the Jacobian \(J\) (the slope) of our error, so that \(\mathbf{c} = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = J^{\top}\mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})\).
    2. Approximate the Hessian: Assume \(Q \approx J^{\top} J\).

    3. Solve the QP: Treat the neighbourhood of \(\mathbf{x}\) as a bowl, and solve the QP,

      \begin{align} \mathrm{QP}(\Delta\mathbf{x}) &= f(\mathbf{x}+\Delta\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{x}) \approx \frac12 \Delta\mathbf{x}^{\top} Q \Delta\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{c}^{\top} \Delta\mathbf{x} \ \Delta\mathbf{x}_ &= -Q^{-1}\mathbf{c} = -(J{\top}J) \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \end{align} J^{\top} 4. Step*: Move to the new position, \(\mathbf{x} \gets \mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x}_*\) and repeat until convergence.

    Convergence Criteria

    Practically, three things are checked to be safe.

    1. Step delta. Is the change if state \(\|\Delta \mathbf{x}_*\|\) effectively zero.
    2. Gradient delta. Is the slope \(\|J^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})\|\) effectively flat?
    3. Cost delta. Did the total error \(|f(\mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x}_*) - f(\mathbf{x})|\) stop decreasing?
    \[\begin{align*} \|\Delta \mathbf{x}_*\| + \|J^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})\| + |f(\mathbf{x} + \Delta\mathbf{x}_*) - f(\mathbf{x})| &< \epsilon \end{align*}\]

    Levenberg Marquardt Algorithm

    The Gauss-Newton method assumes the local “bowl” approximation is valid. But what if \(J^{\top}J\) is singular or the step \(\Delta \mathbf{x}\) is too large? The solver overshoots, and the system diverges. This is where Levenberg and Marquardt stepped in. They added a “damping” term \(\lambda\) to the diagonal of the Hessian:

    \[\begin{align*} (J^{\top} J + \lambda \mathbf{I}) \Delta \mathbf{x}_* &= -J^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \\ \text{or, }\quad \Delta \mathbf{x}_* &= -(J^{\top} J + \lambda I) ^{-1} J^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x}) \end{align*}\]

    The beauty of LM is that it dynamically slides between two optimization strategies based on the value of \(\lambda\):

    When \(\lambda\) is large (The Gradient Descent Limit)

    \(\Delta \mathbf{x}_* \approx -\mathbf{J}^{\top} \mathbf{e} / \lambda\).
    This is a small step in the direction of the steepest descent. It is stable but slow. We use this when our quadratic approximation is poor (the error is increasing).

    When \(\lambda\) is small (The Quadratic Program Limit)

    \(\Delta \mathbf{x}_* \approx (J^{\top}J)^{-1}J^{\top} \mathbf{e}(\mathbf{x})\).
    This is the Gauss-Newton step. It is unstable far from the minimum but lightning-fast (quadratic convergence) near the minimum.

    Comparison with Gauss-Newton
    Feature Standard QP LM Algorithm
    Curvature Exact Hessian (\(H\)) Approximate Hessian, i.e. (\(J^{\top}J+\lambda I\))
    Trust Region Infinite (assumes the bowl is real) Finite (constrained by \(\lambda\))
    Step Calculation One-shot Matrix Inversion Iterative, “Solve, check error, and adjust \(\lambda\)”
    Global Goal Find the bottom of the bowl Move between local bowls until tension is zero
    Strategy for tuning \(\lambda\)

    In SLAM, we use LM because we are constantly moving from “I have no idea where I am” to “I am almost certain where I am.”

    1. Start: \(\lambda\) is high. We take cautious, gradient-like steps to move toward the general area of the minimum.
    2. Middle: As the error decreases, we decrease \(\lambda\). The “springs” of our Factor Graph start behaving more like the Quadratic Model we built.
    3. End: \(\lambda\) becomes near-zero. We take a final, massive “Gauss-Newton leap” to the exact center of the minimum, providing the millimeter-precision needed for AR.

    CV

    To put this in perspective, if SLAM is the “Inner Ear” and “Memory” of an AR system (handling balance, movement, and mapping), then Computer Vision (CV) is the “Eyes” and “Intelligence.”

    While SLAM tells you where you are, CV tells you what you are looking at. Together, they bridge the gap between a raw video feed and a persistent digital world.

    The CV-SLAM Symbiosis

    It is common to say AR = CV + SLAM. Here is how they divide the labor:

    • SLAM: Focuses on geometry. It calculates the transformation matrix between the camera and the world. It doesn’t care if a feature is a “chair” or a “rock”—it only cares that the feature is a 3D point at \((x, y, z)\).
    • Computer Vision: Focuses on semantics and perception. It interprets the image to provide context, constraints, and interaction points for the AR content.

    Key CV Tasks in AR

    Beyond just finding points for SLAM, CV performs several critical functions that make AR feel “real”:

    Image Recognition & Tracking

    This is the ability to detect a specific 2D image (like a movie poster or a QR code) and use it as a “Trigger.”

    • How it works: CV extractors (like SIFT or ORB) create a “fingerprint” of the image and match it against a database.
    • AR Result: When you point your phone at a specific book cover, a 3D animation pops out of it.

    Object Detection & Labeling

    CV identifies real-world objects (cars, people, furniture) using Deep Learning (CNNs).

    Plausible AR Result: A “Terminator-style” HUD that labels the people in your field of view or recognizes a specific model of a machine to overlay repair instructions.

    Semantic Segmentation

    This is the process of labeling every single pixel in a frame (e.g., “this pixel is grass,” “this pixel is sky”).

    Plausible AR Result: This allows for Occlusion. If the CV knows the pixels in the centre of the screen are a “Human,” the AR system can hide a digital monster behind that person instead of just rendering it on top of them.

    Scene Understanding: The “Physics” of CV

    For an AR object to look like it belongs in the room, CV must extract environmental data that SLAM usually ignores:

    • Plane Detection: CV identifies flat surfaces (floors, tables, walls). This is where the Quadratic Program of our SLAM system meets the “Logic” of CV. The system identifies a cluster of 3D points that are co-planar and “snaps” a virtual floor to them.
    • Light Estimation: CV analyzes the highlights and shadows in the camera feed to determine the direction and intensity of real-world light.

    Plausible AR Result: A virtual dinosaur casts a shadow that matches the shadows of the real furniture.

    The “Frontend” of the Pipeline (Feature Extraction)

    Computer Vision provides the raw materials for SLAM. Before you can run a Gauss-Newton optimization, CV must:

    1. Detect: Find “interesting” pixels (corners, blobs).
    2. Describe: Turn those pixels into a math vector (Descriptor).
    3. Match: Compare descriptors between Frame A and Frame B.

    If the CV “Front-end” fails to find good matches (due to motion blur or low light), the SLAM “Back-end” (the optimizer) has no data to work with, and the AR “drifts.”

    Summary: The Two Pillars

    Tech Primary Goal Math/Tooling
    SLAM Spatial Consistency Factor Graphs, Gauss-Newton, IMU Integration
    CV Context & Realism Neural Networks, Feature Matching, Photometry

    In modern AR, the line is blurring. We now have Semantic SLAM, where the “Factor Graph” doesn’t just contain points, but recognized objects (e.g., “The factor between Pose A and Pose B must respect the fact that Pose A is looking at a ‘Table’”).
